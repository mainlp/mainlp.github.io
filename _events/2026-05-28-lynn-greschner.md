title: "Feeling Convinced? The Interplay of Emotions and Convincingess in Arguments"
abstract: "<p>The convincingness of an argument depends on its structure, the person who makes the argument, and the emotion that it causes in the recipient. These emotions do not only depend on the argument itself, but also on the subjective evaluation of the argument's (potential) impact on the self. For instance, an argument to ban plastic bottles might cause fear of losing a job for a bottle industry worker, which lowers the convincingness; presumably independent of its content. The emotional dimension of arguments is underexplored, in parts due to the challenges emerging from the contextualized, subjective, and implicit nature of capturing emotions in text.</p><p>In this talk, I present the intricate interplay between emotions and convincingness in arguments, focusing on the individual subjective evaluation by humans to present an emotion-aware computational framework for subjective argument evaluation. After establishing the basic psychological concepts validated by human studies I present approaches to computationally model such subjective convincingess argument assessments.</p>"
speaker: Lynn Greschner <br/>(PhD Student, University of Bamberg, Germany)
bio: '<p>Lynn Greschner is a third year phd student at the University of Bamberg. Her main research interest are emotions and convincingness in arguments. She’s interested in integrating psychological concepts into NLP, annotator (dis-)agreement, biases and alignment, and perspectivism. Previously she obtained her MSc in computational linguistics at the University of Stuttgart, where she worked on automatic fact checking and biomedical NLP.</p>'

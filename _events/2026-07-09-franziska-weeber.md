title: "Beyond Stated Preferences: Evaluating Explicit and Implicit LLM Alignment"
abstract: "<p>Large Language Models (LLMs) are giving increasingly personalized responses to reflect the preferences, values, and communication styles of different user groups. Such alignment with socio-demographic groups can emerge explicitly through fine-tuning and post-training, or implicitly through adaptation to prompts and conversational context. This talk explores how explicit and implicit alignment of values, opinions, and recommendations can produce unintended behavioral effects that are often overlooked by existing alignment evaluation methods. Explicit interventions can have unintended effects that generalize beyond their targeted behavior. For example, alignment in one language may alter behavior in others. Conversely, prompt-based evaluations may misrepresent real-world behavior, as LLMs often respond more strongly to subtle cues such as writing style and topic than having access to explicit mentions about user attributes. Findings like these underscore the need for alignment evaluations that extend beyond narrow and controlled research settings and reflect the complexity of real-world LLM use.</p>"
speaker: Franziska Weeber <br/>(PhD Student, University of Stuttgart, Germany)
bio: '<p>Franziska Weeber is a research assistant and 3rd year PhD student at the Institute for Natural Language Processing (University of Stuttgart). She is supervised by Prof. Dr. Sebastian Padó. She also is a guest researcher in the Machine Learning group of Prof. Dr. Alan Akbik at Humboldt University Berlin. Her research lies at the intersection of Natural Language Processing and Computational Social Science, focusing on measuring and explaining social and political opinions and biases in large language models. She is particularly interested in LLM value alignment, multilingual and multicultural perspectives, cross-lingual transfer, and the robustness of evaluation methods, as well as annotation and computational social science more broadly.</p>'
